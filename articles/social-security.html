Tax Debate Reignites as Social Security Insolvency Draws Closer

Published June 11, 2026 • Tax Notes

The Social Security Trust Fund remains on track to become insolvent within the next decade, but experts say potential tax-related fixes could improve the program’s increasingly dire financial situation.

The latest estimates, which the fund’s trustees unveiled June 9, anticipate that the program’s reserve funding for old-age recipients will be fully depleted by the end of 2032 — one quarter earlier than projected last year.

A top Social Security Administration official, speaking June 10 at a panel hosted by the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget (CRFB), reiterated that time is running out for lawmakers to take meaningful action.

“We are just about out of time for that ‘sooner,’” said Karen Glenn, the SSA’s chief actuary. “We are at the ‘later.’”

Once the fund hits insolvency, recipients will see a 22 percent reduction in their full benefits, ultimately receiving about 78 cents on the dollar in the first year, the trustees’ report says. That amount would likely drop in subsequent years because of decreasing tax revenue.

This year’s estimates continue the downward trajectory seen in recent years — one that has often been acknowledged by lawmakers but mostly gone unaddressed.

Some lawmakers have become concerned about the looming deadline, with House Ways and Means Committee Chair Jason Smith, R-Mo., urging action during a June 10 committee hearing featuring SSA Commissioner Frank Bisignano.

“We’ve seen nothing in years past, and so I hope our colleagues will come together — both Republican and Democrat — to do it for those people who rely on Social Security,” Smith said. “My mother . . . that’s all she has, that’s all she lives on, and that’s difficult to do. There’s a lot of people like that, and that’s why we have to come together.”

Payroll Tax in the Crosshairs

Despite calls for bipartisan action, Congress has long struggled to come to a consensus on the path forward.

“It’s a simple math problem, [but] it’s a difficult political problem,” Glenn said, later adding, “The solutions are straightforward: You need to add revenue or lower costs.”

Most options floated by tax professionals center on the payroll tax, of which 6.2 percent of a worker’s income — up to an income limit, currently set at $184,500 — is earmarked for Social Security benefits. That 6.2 percent is matched by employers, totaling 12.4 percent.

Proposals to raise the payroll tax or eliminate the cap, which would generate more funds from higher-income earners, have long been pushed by Democrats. Republicans have instead sought reforms to the program itself, such as a change in the retirement age, as opposed to raising taxes.

The CRFB, however, has developed a new option. While keeping the 6.2 percent payroll tax paid by workers, the employer’s portion would instead be replaced by an “employer compensation tax,” a flat tax the think tank claims would “significantly improve” trust fund solvency.

If implemented, the initiative would broaden the tax base, according to Mark Sarney, the CRFB’s director of Social Security policy, who said the percentage of nonwage employee benefits, such as insurance or transit subsidies, is growing but failing to provide Social Security with additional tax revenue.

“It’s a more efficient way of raising revenue, and it doesn’t fall on the worker. . . . They won’t see a tax increase in their own paycheck,” Sarney said.

However, the proposed employer compensation tax — as well as most other singular options — would shrink only a portion of the overall gap, Sarney added. “You pretty much need a package of multiple options,” he said.

Back to the Future

Instead of coming up with new solutions themselves, two House lawmakers are vouching for the same approach taken by lawmakers the last time Social Security insolvency was a major issue.

Rep. Tom Cole, R-Okla., and Ways and Means member Thomas R. Suozzi, D-N.Y., introduced a bill June 8 that would establish a 13-member independent commission that would present a plan to address Social Security insolvency within a year.

The proposal is specifically modeled after the National Commission on Social Security Reform, which was established in 1981. That commission’s findings led to the Social Security Amendments of 1983, which made sweeping changes to the program and ultimately remedied the short-term funding problem.

“Everyone knows Social Security faces serious long-term challenges, but too often Washington chooses to ignore them,” Suozzi said in a release. “We owe it to current and future retirees to set partisan politics aside and develop responsible solutions that protect and strengthen the program.”
