House Committee Investigating Federal Employees’ Tax Delinquency

Published June 26, 2026 • Tax Notes

The U.S. government would get a 50 percent equity stake in the country’s largest artificial intelligence companies under a tax proposed by Senate Finance Committee member Bernie Sanders, I-Vt.

The American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act, introduced June 18 by Sanders, would implement the one-time tax with the intent of netting a say in the decision-making of AI companies, as well as a monetary benefit, for everyday Americans.

“The goal is not complicated,” Sanders told reporters. “AI is built on the foundation of human knowledge, but the work of millions and millions of people. Every tweet that you send out, every email that you send out, every article that you write, that’s part of AI. The American people should then be able to stop what’s bad and benefit from the financial gains.”

The tax would apply only to new AI companies that reach $200 million in annual AI sales, the bill says. As only the AI portion of companies would be affected, businesses that are tax-eligible would be forced to split into AI and non-AI entities.

Under the proposal, the government’s equity shares would be placed into a public wealth fund overseen by an independent seven-member commission. Commissioners would be nominated by the president and subject to Senate confirmation, with the panel being ultimately responsible for acting in the best interests of the public while on a company’s board.

“Will they be perfect? I’m sure they will not, but that is a hell of a lot better,” said Sanders, who added that the commission would provide public accountability that the industry lacks. “The idea that 50 percent of the board is controlled by the public, that we can stop bad decisions . . . is a significant step forward.”

The fund’s potential value is estimated at $7 trillion, according to projections from Sanders’s office. An annual dividend of 5 percent would be disbursed from the fund, initially netting each American a direct payment of at least $1,000 — an amount Sanders sees increasing in subsequent years “as AI becomes more prosperous.”

“As the wealth of AI grows, it could mean significant amounts of money coming into social programs, making sure that all Americans have healthcare, education, decent housing and other basic necessities alike,” Sanders said.

Long Road Ahead

Sanders, who has long championed causes aligned with raising tax rates on the ultrawealthy to make them pay “their fair share,” is no stranger to introducing long-shot legislation.

But when it comes to reining in AI, Sanders says he’s not the only one on Capitol Hill seeking to regulate the industry.

“I think there is within the Senate and within the House — belatedly, much too late — a growing understanding of the consequences of AI and the need to do something,” Sanders said.

Sanders told reporters that a lack of public support for regulation in the industry is because companies “have a gun at our heads.”

“They have the ability to spend huge amounts of money in campaigns to defeat any candidate who is talking about sensible regulation or actions that will benefit the public,” Sanders said.
