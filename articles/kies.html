Departure of Kies Adds to List of Treasury, IRS Vacancies

Published July 15, 2026 • Tax Notes

Treasury’s highest-ranking tax official intends to leave the department soon, subsequently leaving two top tax jobs vacant.

Kenneth Kies, who has served as both Treasury assistant secretary of tax policy and acting IRS chief counsel since last summer, is expected to end his tenure at the department, an administration official confirmed to Tax Notes July 14. His final day, however, isn’t yet known.

Confirmed to lead Treasury’s Office of Tax Policy in June 2025, Kies oversaw the implementation of and regulatory agenda for the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, which became law just days after the start of his tenure.

Kies has also served as acting IRS chief counsel since July 2025, overseeing about 2,000 lawyers on the agency’s side of the bill’s implementation alongside Audrey Morris, IRS deputy chief counsel (operations). He also led an initiative to send one final round of settlement offers to taxpayers benefiting from exorbitant conservation easements.

“Ken came in to help the Trump administration with the Working Families Tax Cuts, implementation of the most successful tax season and Trump accounts,” the administration official said. “We appreciate his service to our nation.”

Kies’s announced departure follows the reported departures of numerous other Treasury officials in recent weeks, including Derek Theurer, deputy undersecretary for legislative affairs, and Jonathan McKernan, undersecretary for domestic finance.

Treasury also remains without a full-time deputy secretary following the resignation of Michael Faulkender in August. Francis Brooke, a former counselor to Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, has been nominated for the role, and the Senate Finance Committee is scheduled to hold a hearing on his nomination July 16.

Tax Policy Staffing

The Trump administration is expected to consider internal Treasury candidates to succeed Kies, one person familiar with the process told Tax Notes, but it is unclear how fruitful that route will be given the state of staffing within the Office of Tax Policy.

Three of the five deputy assistant secretary jobs within the office either are vacant or feature an acting officeholder. Only Kevin Salinger, who leads tax policy, and Rebecca Oakes Burch, who leads international tax affairs, are full-time appointees, according to Treasury’s website.

It’s unknown whether either one will be under consideration to succeed Kies as assistant secretary for tax policy. A request for insight into the nomination process sent to a White House spokesperson wasn’t returned.

David Kautter of RSM US LLP, a former Treasury assistant secretary for tax policy and acting IRS commissioner, said Salinger is among the likely candidates to replace Kies on an interim basis, but finding a long-term replacement could be a challenge.

“Ken leaves behind a strong staff, including a strong deputy assistant secretary,” Kautter, who is also a member of the Tax Analysts board of directors, said. “Getting a new assistant secretary confirmed would take a while in the current environment and could take even longer depending on the outcome of the midterm elections.”

Perhaps to the detriment of Salinger’s potential candidacy, however, is his apparent enemy in President Trump's ally and conservative influencer Laura Loomer, who has taken credit for derailing the nomination of Donald Korb to be IRS chief counsel last year. Loomer previously referred to Salinger as a “Radical Left [Barack] Obama Donor Who Helped LGBTQ and HIV Positive Immigrants Get Assylum [sic] In The US.”

Show Goes On

Kies’s departure from the Office of Tax Policy isn’t expected to have a major impact on Treasury’s ongoing tax regulatory agenda, which includes a spread of outstanding proposed regulations and guidance relating to the OBBBA.

“Projects that were going to go forward — they’ll go forward,” said Monte A. Jackel of Jackel Tax Law, a former Treasury and chief counsel attorney. “I don’t know whether his loss on a personal level will have an impact.”

Still, the move adds to a year and a half of consistent turnover in Treasury and IRS leadership, including the rotation of seven different IRS commissioners before the appointment of Frank Bisignano as IRS CEO.

Former IRS Commissioner John Koskinen said that leaving the assistant secretary for tax policy, chief counsel, and commissioner roles vacant at the same time could pose “a real challenge” for policymakers who rely on chain of command.

One solution would be to expedite the confirmation of a new chief counsel, according to Koskinen. “That gets you the IRS half; you still need the discussion on the Treasury side,” he said.

Kies was originally slated to remain in charge of IRS chief counsel until Trump’s newest pick for the job, James Gadwood, is confirmed. Gadwood, an attorney at Miller & Chevalier Chtd., has no shortage of experience in the tax controversy space and is likely to face some scrutiny during the nomination process for work he did for DJT Holdings LLC, one of Trump’s holding companies.

Among the possible candidates for acting chief counsel in Kies’s absence are Morris and career attorney William Paul. Paul has been acting chief counsel for several different stints — including one that lasted nearly three years — but was pushed out of the role by the Trump administration in favor of Andrew De Mello, putting his prospects for another chance in question.

De Mello, a Trump ally who was quietly replaced by Kies after a brief stint as acting chief counsel, was nominated July 13 to be a Tax Court judge.

The IRS didn’t respond to a request for comment by press time.
